Context: Two Fiscal Pictures of Berkeley

Source: City Auditor Report (May 2022) + HSPE Agenda Package (May 2026)

Jenny Wong’s 2022 audit found Berkeley’s near-term finances strong — revenues grew 25% (inflation-adjusted) and outpaced expenses in 8 of 10 years. But long-term risks loomed: pension liabilities hit $658M, OPEB grew 91%, and unfunded infrastructure needs reached $1.2 billion.

Health & Welfare spending — the category that includes homeless services — saw the second-largest real growth of any function at 65%, rising from $25.2M to $41.6M. Today, the 33 homeless programs tracked in the HSPE agenda total $21.7M annually, representing a significant and growing share of that category.

Key Insight: Health & Welfare grew 65% in real terms over the decade — and homeless services are a major driver. At $21.7M, today’s homeless programs alone approach the entire FY 2012 Health & Welfare budget of $25.2M. This spending category is on a trajectory that outpaces revenue growth (25% over the same period).

The Squeeze: Berkeley faces $1.97 billion in combined unfunded obligations (capital + pension + OPEB) while spending $21.7M/year on homeless programs serving ~4,400+ individuals. The City’s pension funded ratio at 66% is rated “high risk” by the State Auditor. Every dollar committed to growing homeless services competes with these legacy obligations.
